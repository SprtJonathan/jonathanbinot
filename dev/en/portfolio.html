Source² Engine (Half-Life: Alyx - VR game)

The Source² engine, although very new, shares many aspects of its predecessor, on which I have spent a lot of time over the past few years, learning Level Design techniques. I enjoyed going back on it and deepening my basics. It is a tool that I find very pleasant to use.
The following example is a map for the game Half-Life Alyx that I have been working on for a month. I continue my engine learning process as I progress on the geometry of the map. I learned to use some of the tools built into the engine:
- Tile Meshing: 3D models programmed to form user-defined paths (e.g. pipes to walls, air ducts etc.)
- 3D Skyboxes: use of an external map in order to "project" it on the current map in order to optimize the render of the 3D models by increasing their size.
- Scripted Sequences (Entities allowing to script character animations in order to make them perform a defined action)
- Dynamic lighting

This video shows the beginning of one of the maps I made. The pipes are placed with the tile meshing tool.

This one shows scripted sequences. The creature (a headcrab) that comes from behind the pillar is "called" by and entity that makes it spawn on the map, and another entity named "scripted sequence" makes it do the animation I chose. Once this animation is done, the default AI of the NPC takes the control back.

Another example of a scripted sequence. Here, the zombie (the name of these NPCs in the Half-Life franchise) has an idle animation set (it is pointing the light with its finger). Once the player touches a trigger I placed in the map, the animation of the pointing stops and the zombie has to walk to the second scripted sequence. When he arrives at the second entity, an output of this entity makes it go ragdoll (its body is free of animation) and once he drops to the ground, another output sets its health points to 0.

The last example of a scripted sequence. This time, the when the player approaches the door, a trigger triggers the soldiers walk as well as the radio sound that plays behind the door.

Unreal Engine 4

The Unreal Engine 4 might be the game engine on which I spend most of my time learning game dev in general. While I was concentrating on level design with the Source Engine, the UE4 allowed me to learn how to code and what was the logic behind simple programs thanks to the blueprints visual coding system. By following some online courses, I learnt how to create a small 3D platform game (Knuckles Strike), that according to the images, looks like a strange joke.
More recently, I started to learn how to create VR projects in UE4. I'm only starting to scratch the surface, but I progress every day, and I learn fast.

This video shows the first game I finished : It's based on a course I followed about creating a 3D platform game. The version I made differs from the example with the 3D models and the sounds I used. I asked some friends to make exagerated sounds to get an absurd result.

For a small period of time (approximately 2 months), I joined small team of people willing to develop a project based on remaking some elements of Half-Life 2 on Unreal Engine 4. I worked on some aspects of the project, mainly the level design, and the map that is shown here is an example of what I worked on.

For fun and to discover other game dev disciplines, I tried to animate the Half-Life² soldiers 3D model with the online auto rigger and animator : Mixamo.

This video shows a bug that happened when I imported the animations of the zombie from Half-Life² in UE4. During my experimentations, I often faced similar problems. This one comes from the fact that Source games models animations are not fixed in place, while Unreal system of actor is.
